<p>The message included a QR code with a Bitcoin address that users were invited to send coins, after which they would receive twice the amount.</p>
<p>The rest of the website’s functionality at the time was limited so that users couldn’t get past the message.</p>

<p></p>
<p>By press time, the <a href="https://www.blockchain.com/en/btc/address/1NgoFwgsfZ19RrCUhTmmuLpmdek45nRd5N" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="sc-18vyx5u-0 hiPYeO">address</a> received a total of nine transactions worth 0.40571238 Bitcoin (about $17,800 in current prices), with some users on Twitter suggesting that those could be self transactions to make an illusion of legitimate activity.</p>
<!-- -->
<p>Promoting giveaway scams has been a popular tactic among cybercriminals in recent years. They often impersonate well-known persons and celebrities, with the likes of Tesla CEO Elon Musk, Microsoft founder Bill Gates, and Ethereum co-founder Vitalik Buterin among the names used by hackers.</p>
<p>Bitcoin.org is the oldest crypto-related website that was registered by <a href="https://decrypt.co/resources/satoshi-nakamoto" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="sc-18vyx5u-0 hiPYeO">Satoshi Nakamoto</a> and Martti Malmi back in 2008. It’s not affiliated with the Bitcoin Foundation, a non-profit organization founded in 2012.</p>

<p>Namecheap, the hosting provider for Bitcoin.org, was alerted as well, and it didn’t take the company long to temporarily disable the domain, winning plaudits for prompt action.</p>

<p></p>
<p>To add to the tension, Cøbra, the current pseudonymous operator of Bitcoin.org, reacted to the incident only a couple of hours later, <a href="https://twitter.com/CobraBitcoin/status/1440905892543832064" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="sc-18vyx5u-0 hiPYeO">confirming</a> that the website was compromised and that the incident is being investigated.</p>
<p>While the exact way the hackers managed to compromise Bitcoin.org remains unclear, Cøbra suggested that attackers exploited a flaw in the DNS configuration after the website moved to Cloudflare two months ago.</p>

<p>By press time, Bitcoin.org looks to be accessible again, however, the <a href="https://bitcoin.org/bitcoin.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="sc-18vyx5u-0 hiPYeO">direct URL</a> for the Bitcoin white paper hosted on the platform shows a &quot;This site can’t be reached” message.</p>
<p>In June this year, after Cøbra chose to preserve his pseudonymity rather than defend himself against charges of copyright infringement, Bitcoin.org lost a legal battle against the self-proclaimed Bitcoin inventor Craig Wright, with a UK court <a href="https://decrypt.co/74657/bitcoin-inventor-whitepaper-lawsuit-craig-wright-cobra" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="sc-18vyx5u-0 hiPYeO">ordering</a> the website to remove the Bitcoin white paper for UK visitors.</p>
<p>Shortly after the legal defeat, Bitcoin.org was hit with a <a href="https://decrypt.co/75276/bitcoin-org-reportedly-hit-ddos-attack-ransom-demand" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="sc-18vyx5u-0 hiPYeO">severe DDos attack</a>, with attackers demanding a ransom of 0.5 BTC.</p>
